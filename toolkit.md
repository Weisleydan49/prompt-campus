# Personalized AI Prompt Toolkit — BSc Computer Science

A reusable set of prompts for my degree. Built so that one block describes *me* once, and the rest are ready-to-use prompts for almost everything I'll do across a semester.

**How to use this:**
1. Fill in the **Master Context Block** once per unit (it takes two minutes).
2. When you want to do something, copy the matching prompt below, paste the Master Context Block at the top of it, fill any extra `[brackets]`, and attach your materials.
3. Save a filled-in copy per unit so you're not rewriting it each week.

Anything in `[square brackets]` is for you to fill in. Delete lines that don't apply.

---

## Master Context Block (fill once per unit)

> **About me:** I'm a [second-year] BSc Computer Science student at Kenyatta University. My background in this specific topic is [none / weak / moderate / strong]. The unit is [unit name and code, e.g. CSC 201 Data Structures and Algorithms]. My institution uses [citation style if relevant, e.g. IEEE] and I'm assessed by [exams / coursework / labs / group project — note the weighting if you know it].
>
> **How I learn best:** [Give me concrete examples or code before abstract definitions. / Ask me questions rather than lecturing. / Use everyday analogies. / Break things into small steps and check I've understood before moving on. / Keep jargon defined as you go.]
>
> **Ground rule:** Don't do my graded work for me. Help me understand, plan, and improve my own work so I stay within my university's academic integrity policy. Tell me when you're filling gaps with general knowledge versus working from my materials.

Paste that at the top of any prompt below.

## 1. Generate simplified notes from material

Use when a unit is dense and you want clean, study-ready notes from a reading, slide deck, or your own messy notes.

> Turn the attached [lecture slides / textbook chapter / my rough notes] into simplified, study-ready notes for [unit].
> - Use plain language and define every technical term the first time it appears.
> - Organize by topic with short headings, and keep explanations tight.
> - For each key idea, add a one-line "why it matters" and, where useful, a small worked example or code snippet.
> - End with a 5-point "must-remember" summary and 3 things students commonly get wrong here.
> Base everything on my attached material; flag anything you add from outside it.

---

## 2. Explain a concept until I actually understand it

Use when you're stuck on a single idea and want depth, not a one-line definition.

> Explain [concept, e.g. Big-O notation / pointers / normalization / recursion] to me as if you're my tutor.
> - Start with an intuition or analogy, then the precise definition, then a concrete example (code or diagram-in-text if relevant).
> - Show me a common wrong mental model and why it's wrong.
> - Then ask me 2 questions to check I've got it, and correct me based on my answers before moving on.
> Don't move past a step until I confirm I understand it.

---

## 3. Summarize / condense concepts

Use when you've understood something and want a compact version for revision.

> Summarize [topic / these notes / this chapter] for [unit] into a concise revision sheet.
> - Give me the core idea in 2–3 sentences first.
> - Then the key sub-points as short bullets, with any formulas, syntax, or definitions I must memorize clearly marked.
> - Keep it to roughly [half a page / one page].
> Don't add new material — only condense what's in the source.

---

## 4. Pull a specific concept out of a large PDF

Use when you have a big document (textbook, spec, research paper) and only need one part.

> I've attached a large [textbook / specification / paper]. I only need to learn about [specific concept, e.g. "B-tree insertion" or "the TCP three-way handshake"].
> - Find where this is covered and extract just that.
> - Explain it in simplified terms using [unit]-level language for a [year] student.
> - Tell me which section/page it came from so I can read the original if needed.
> - If the document only covers part of it, say what's missing and fill the gap from reliable general knowledge, clearly marked.

---

## 5. Group assignment help

Use to plan, divide, and integrate a group project without doing your teammates' thinking for them.

> I'm working on a group [assignment / project] for [unit]. The task is: [paste the brief]. We are [number] members. Act as our planning facilitator.
> - Break the task into clear work packages and suggest a fair way to divide them by skill or interest.
> - Give us a timeline working back from the [deadline], with checkpoints.
> - Flag the parts where the pieces have to fit together (interfaces, shared formats, naming) so we don't clash at integration.
> - Suggest how to review each other's work before submission.
> Help us organize and reason about the work — don't write the deliverable itself.

---

## 6. Individual assignment / essay help

Use for written or design coursework where the work must stay yours.

> I have an individual assignment for [unit]: [paste the brief and any rubric]. Act as a coach, not a ghostwriter.
> - First, help me interpret what's actually being asked and what the rubric rewards.
> - Help me build an outline and decide what to include.
> - When I share a draft, critique it against the rubric — point out weak arguments, gaps, and unclear bits — but let me do the rewriting.
> Don't write the assignment for me.

---

## 7. Understand existing code

Use when you've been given code (a sample, a library, a teammate's module) and need to follow it.

> Walk me through this code from [unit / a project]. [Paste or attach the code.]
> - Explain what it does overall in one paragraph first.
> - Then go through it in logical chunks, explaining each in plain language.
> - Point out any patterns, idioms, or tricks a beginner wouldn't recognize.
> - End by asking me to predict what [a specific line / function] does, so I can test my understanding.

---

## 8. Debug — and learn from it

Use when something's broken and you want to fix it *and* not repeat the mistake.

> My code for [unit] isn't working. [Paste the code, the error, and what I expected vs what happened.]
> - Help me find the bug by reasoning through it, ideally guiding me to spot it rather than just handing me the fix.
> - Once we find it, explain *why* it happened and the underlying concept I was missing.
> - Suggest how I'd catch this class of bug faster next time.

---

## 9. Learn a new language, tool, or framework

Use when a unit throws a new technology at you.

> I need to learn [language / tool / framework, e.g. SQL / Git / React] for [unit], starting from [no experience / some experience]. Build me a learning path.
> - Start with the core concepts I must understand before touching syntax.
> - Give me small, increasing-difficulty exercises, and check my answers as I go.
> - Use examples relevant to [my unit / a project I'm building].
> - After each stage, quiz me before we advance.

---

## 10. Practice questions & exam prep

Use to drill actively instead of re-reading.

> Act as my exam coach for [unit]. My exam covers [topics] and the format is [MCQ / short answer / coding / proofs / mixed].
> - Generate practice questions matching that format and difficulty.
> - Ask them one at a time, wait for my answer, then mark it, explain the correct reasoning, and give a harder follow-up if I got it right.
> - Track which topics I keep missing and tell me where to focus.
> Base questions on my attached [notes / past papers] where provided.

---

## 11. Math- and proof-heavy units

Use for discrete math, algorithm analysis, theory of computation, etc.

> Help me with [topic, e.g. proof by induction / solving recurrences / set theory] for [unit].
> - Show me the general method first, then one fully worked example with every step justified.
> - Then give me a similar problem to solve myself, and check my working line by line — point out exactly where I go wrong, not just the final answer.
> - Don't skip steps as "obvious"; I'm still learning the notation.

---

## 12. Lab reports & project documentation

Use for write-ups where structure and clarity are graded.

> I need to write a [lab report / project documentation] for [unit] about [what I did]. [Attach the brief/template.]
> - Help me structure it according to the required format.
> - Ask me what I did at each stage and help me phrase it clearly and technically.
> - Critique my draft for clarity, completeness, and correct terminology.
> The technical content and results are mine — help me present them well.

---

## 13. Compare concepts or technologies

Use when a unit asks "which, when, and why."

> Compare [A] and [B] for [unit, e.g. "stacks vs queues" or "SQL vs NoSQL"].
> - Give me a short comparison table of the key differences.
> - Then explain when you'd choose each and why, with a concrete example for each.
> - End by asking me to pick the right one for a scenario you give me, then check my reasoning.

---

## 14. Build a study / revision plan

Use at the start of a unit or before exams.

> Help me build a [weekly study plan / exam revision schedule] for [unit(s)]. I have [hours available per week] and the [exam/deadline] is on [date]. My weak areas are [topics].
> - Prioritize my weak areas and the highest-weighted topics.
> - Mix in active recall and practice, not just reading.
> - Lay it out as a realistic schedule I can actually follow, with rest built in.

---

## 15. Presentation prep

Use when you have to present a project or topic.

> I'm presenting [topic] for [unit] to [audience] in [time limit]. Help me prepare.
> - Help me structure the talk so it flows and fits the time.
> - Suggest what to put on slides versus what to say.
> - Then act as the audience: ask me likely questions so I can practice answering.
> The content is mine — help me sharpen and deliver it.

---

## Quick tips that apply to all of these

- **Attach your real materials.** A prompt working from *your* syllabus, notes, or rubric is far more personalized than one working from general knowledge.
- **Correct the AI immediately.** "Too advanced," "simpler," "use a CS example," "more depth on the mechanism" — each correction tunes it to you within that chat.
- **Keep one chat per unit per topic.** It remembers the context within a conversation, so you build up a tailored tutor over time.
- **Treat the first reply as a draft.** The real value comes from the back-and-forth, not the first answer.
