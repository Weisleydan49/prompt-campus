**1. Establish context about yourself first.**
Tell the AI your level, program, and background so it pitches explanations correctly. "I'm a second-year nursing student who hasn't taken chemistry since high school" gets you a very different (and more useful) explanation than just "explain pharmacokinetics." Include your institution's expectations if relevant, like the citation style your program uses or the rubric you're graded on.

**2. State your learning goal, not just the task.**
There's a difference between "summarize this chapter" and "help me understand this chapter well enough to apply it in a case study exam." The first gets you a summary you'll forget; the second gets the AI to quiz you, give examples, and check your reasoning. Always say what you need to be able to *do* with the material.

**3. Describe how you learn best.**
This is the heart of personalization. Tell it things like: "Explain concepts with real clinical examples before giving definitions," "I learn best when you ask me questions instead of lecturing," "Use analogies from everyday life," or "Break things into steps and check my understanding before moving on." The AI will follow these instructions consistently if you state them.

**4. Set the role and the format.**
Assign the AI a role that matches your need: "Act as a patient tutor preparing me for my pathophysiology exam" or "Act as a Socratic discussion partner who challenges my arguments." Then specify the output format: practice questions, a study plan, a step-by-step walkthrough, flashcard-style Q&A, or plain prose.

**5. Give it your actual materials.**
Personalization works best when the AI works from *your* syllabus, lecture notes, rubric, or readings rather than generic knowledge. Upload or paste them and say "base your explanations on these notes" so it aligns with what your professor actually teaches.

**6. Ask it to adapt, not just answer.**
Build feedback loops into the prompt: "Quiz me, and when I get something wrong, explain why and give me a similar question," or "After each section, ask me to explain it back in my own words and correct my misconceptions." This turns a one-shot answer into an actual tutoring session.

**7. Set boundaries for academic integrity.**
A good personalization prompt includes what the AI should *not* do: "Don't write my assignment for me — help me outline, critique my draft, and explain concepts I'm stuck on." This keeps the AI as a learning tool rather than a shortcut that undermines your learning (and your school's policies).

**8. Iterate.**
Your first prompt is a draft. If the response is too advanced, too shallow, or too generic, say so specifically: "Simpler language," "More depth on mechanism," "Use a nursing example instead." Each correction teaches the AI your preferences within that conversation.

Here's a template that pulls it all together:

> "You are my personal tutor for [course]. I'm a [year/program] student, and my background in this topic is [strong/weak/none]. My goal is to [pass an exam / write a paper / apply this clinically]. I learn best when you [teaching style preferences]. Use my attached [notes/syllabus/rubric] as the basis. Start by assessing what I already know with a few questions, then teach me the gaps step by step, checking my understanding as we go. Don't do my graded work for me — guide my thinking instead."
